Pink Bikini

Apr. 28, 1987

I'd been living in Willow Glen for a couple years. It was a reasonably nice part of San Jose with older houses.  Ours had a long kitchen running half the length of its east side.  The lino, countertop tiles, cabinets and walls were shades of yellow whose original disagreements could be seen in the places least touched by morning sun but generally aged to a rental resignation. 
 The driveway was along that side of the house. My Firebird was in the detached garage keeping my housemate's pot plants company. Cross-adulterating, the car's interior smelled like indica and the bud tasted faintly of gasoline and tires. 
 Next door was a house the mirror layout of ours, so the driveways ran adjacent. It had been on the market and recently bought by a smarmy guy who hung an Armenian flag and insurance sign and replaced the front lawn with concrete so his customers had somewhere to park. No street parking on Curtner Avenue. 
 That day I pulled up on my motorcycle, clicked it into neutral and thumbed the kill switch to make my silent glide toward the back.  While gliding, I would usually stand on the left foot peg and swing my right leg over the seat so that when I feathered the brake to a stop I'd flick the kickstand down and step away, much as I'd do on a bicycle.  But I stopped at the front because of the woman in the bright pink bikini with the little watering can sprinkling the flowers recently planted in the dirt strip between the driveways. The flowers paled. 
 She sashayed from the Armenian's driveway to mine, and put the tip of her index finger on the top edge of my bike's mirror and said: "Nice." 
 Pulling off my helmet gave me a few seconds. I said: "It doesn't need watering." 
 "How 'bout you?" She raised her can. 
 "I'm good for now." 
 Her smile sprouted horns. "Will you give me a ride?" 
 Motorcycles did draw women and this would momentarily baffle me because when a guy gives another guy a ride there was usually an attempt to wheelie and dump your friend. It's in the Y chromosome rulebook somewhere. 
 "Sure," I said. Next to Mating Rituals is the section on Gallantry Toward Women. Not only didn't you try to dump them on the road or trail, you tried to protect them. And that meant gear.  I had a spare leather jacket and gloves. I had a helmet that would fit her, well enough anyway.  "Gotta wear jeans and shoes, though." 
 Parts tilted subtly this way and that and her free hand swept to showcase her current state of attire. "No?" 
 "Only in my dreams." Of which. 
 "Okay. When?" 
 "Knock on the back door. I've got to check my answering machine." I did not. But I needed to find a helmet that didn't smell like perfume. My last pillion jockey was a real spritzer. 
 She leapt the flowers she'd been watering and loped toward the back of the Armenian's in her flip flops. I was in motion before I heard his back door open and close. Might be two minutes, maybe an hour. 
 My helmet collection was in the garage, but also I wanted access to my car for later.  I didn't want this woman to see the potted plants so I quickly hauled open the big door and pushed my Firebird out. A lot of wear and tear starting a cold motor, and in those days I enjoyed that exercise, on flat ground anyway. I had this idea it kept me honest or appreciative or something.  Back inside the garage I closed the big door behind me.  From the grow lights I could see my collection of brain buckets.  I selected one of the smalls and exitted via the side door. 
 And there she was. Jeans, tennies, tee, and a small duffel. I closed the door against the wafting weediness, for the nth and most acute time aware it needed a lock. 
 "Can I drop this somewhere?" She casually lifted the duffel. 
 "Sure. C'mon inside. We'll get you a jacket." 
 "It's summer," she said. 
 "Almost. But I have a policy with motorcycles. All the gear, every time." 
 "Cautious," she said. 
 "Gotta be. Old cars drip oil at intersections, a braking hazard for sudden red lights."  At the back door of the house now. "Drivers turn left in front of bikes all the time, more panic braking."  I checked the lower glass panes of the door before opening it. My little black cat was eyeing the stranger.  "Back up, Fred." He did. 
 My bedroom was immediately to the left with an identical door, odd for a bedroom. I'd hung a random tablecloth on the inside. Spartan. A big bin of clean clothes, big bin for laundry, a pair of twin-sized box springs and mattresses shoved together. I slept diagonally to keep from pushing them apart.  It's a habit I return to when circumstances allow, required or not. I pulled my old bomber from the closet and turned to hand it to her. 
 But she was looking down. "Hi, Fred," she said. I realized I liked her voice. 
 "Fred," I said, "shoo." He did a lithe feline uey. 
 I held out the jacket and she put it on. The sleeves were long so I folded back the cuffs. Then I handed her a pair of gloves. She pulled one on. 
 "Uh," I said, "my mistake. Let's try the helmet." Unless your gloves fit perfectly it's impossible to fasten a helmet strap.  Neat ears and an oval head, so it slid on easily.  She had that look of surprise at the novelty, the change in sounds. Doing the strap brought us close. 
 "Shake your head a bit." Not too much slop. "Good to go." I lead us out. 
 "Bye, Fred," she said. 
 At the front of the house I walked the bike through a three-point turn so it was pointed at the Armenian's little parking lot. I folded down the passenger footpegs and she promptly went to sling a leg over the seat but I stopped her with a hand on her shoulder. Another touch, procedural again, but still.  "Driver first." I got on, flicked the kickstand and said, "Okay." 
 She put her foot on the peg and her hands on my shoulders and swung herself aboard. The rear suspension sagged with the extra weight but only an inch. I tended toward stiff settings. 
 I started the bike just as my housemate pulled into the drive in his ancient VW Fastback. He stopped. I waved and she did too.  I pushed back so the rear tire was touching our lawn and the VW edged past us and down the driveway, so slowly I figured he was watching us in his mirror. 
 I eased the bike across our driveway, between a couple bunches of the Armenian's flowers in the dirt strip, then acrross his driveway and rode a diagonal across his new concrete and curb cut, and out onto Curtner.  In case he was watching, I suppose. Any good raid involves a trespass. 
 We were only three houses from the light and it was red. "Lean into corners with me," I said over my shoulder. 
 "Okay." Her arms were already around my waist, hands clasped. While waiting for green, she unclasped them and put her palms on my thighs. 
 The cross traffic light went yellow and I said, "Okay." She clasped her hands again. 
 I drove through downtown Willow Glen thinking about a coffee shop but it was too close. So I headed for Los Gatos. She was leaning correctly in turns. Curving onto the highway ramp I said, "Hold on!"  I felt her grip tighten. I gave her what, in my experience, women seem to want from a motorcycle ride.  We merged at ninety, a limit set by thrill-me-but-don't-kill-me, a syndrome originating in infants' memories of being tossed in the air by their fathers, something I'd never seen a mother do, undoubtably due to greater investment.  I checked my mirror and over my shoulder, caught a glimpse of her smiling in the wind, and shifted left to the fast lane. I slowed a bit as I recalled having had to switch the tank to its reserve on my way home which meant we'd left with a little under a half gallon of fuel.  We were halfway through that, but Los Gatos wasn't far. We'd make it, but would have to stop.  So we did, at the Chevron, my preferred brand. Expensive, but had tested best for burning cleanly. 
 She seemed to intuit the dismount procedure, but when I felt her hands on my shoulders I said, "Hold on."  I felt some squirrelliness under my right sole, grit and oil. It wouldn't do to topple in front of the general public. I eased the bike backward with my left foot and felt with my right again.  Better. "Okay." 
 She hopped off, as neatly as any. I liked her. I wondered if she was still wearing the bikini under her clothes. 
 I pulled off one glove to facilitate popping the gas cap. She started to emulate but I said, "Don't bother, this won't take long." I was a familiar customer to the guy inside, so I waved and he enabled the pump. I filled the tank, and closed the cap. "Be one sec," I said and went inside to pay. 
 "Running her up 9?" the guy inside asked, referring to the nearby windy road popular with bikers. 
 I paused while fishing an appropriate bill from my wallet. "Dunno." 
 He said, "Heard there was a wreck up there earlier." 
 "While ago?" 
 "Not long." 
 "Good to know. Thanks much." 
 He saluted and handed me my change. 
 At the bike I told her: "There's a problem on Highway 9. I'm thinking we should change plans." 
 "There were plans?" she said. 
 Maybe it was because her helmet was Yamaha yellow, but her eyes were very blue. 
 I thought of three different things to say and felt mature for rejecting them. "Do you like seafood?" 
 Horns. "Like what?" 
 I pointed to a place down the street. "Oysters Rockefeller." 
 I spun us over there, parked and we got seats at the bar. 
 Their arrival raised an eyebrow, but they were very pretty, lovely shells on a bed of rock salt, and when she saw me eat one she quickly followed suit. Her expression was like watching a kaleidoscope that settled on fascination with what had happened in her mouth. I ordered a glass of dry riesling for her. 
 When she'd eaten her last oyster, she asked: "Can I have one of these shells?" 
 "That's how they come. They don't reuse them. Wipe it clean, though, before you put it in your pocket." 
 I watched her pick her favorite and lick a smidge of cheese from its edge. Nice hands, no polish.  Nice lips, also bare. 
 I ran us back to Willow Glen. It was dark now. The area in front of the garage was a bit crowded with my Firebird, the Fastback, and my other housemate's Honda Civic.  But there's always room to park a bike. We went inside where housemates and cats were watching TV in a room maybe intended for a dining room table. Four heads turned when we came in. 
 "Hey," I said to them, and opened my bedroom door. 
 She waved. Fred gave her the slow blink. She hadn't even fed him anything. Mr. Fastback was the pot grower and he just looked. Mr. Civic, a water polo coach who had California written all over him, gave us a shaka sign. He seemed to share Fred's opinion. 
 In my bedroom I tossed my helmet and jacket and gloves on the mattresses, noting her duffel there.  I helped her again undo the D-ring strap closure on the helmet. 
 "Need a bathroom break?" It had been a few hours. 
 "Um—okay." 
 I opened the other door in my odd study/bedroom. The bathroom door was a few feet down the hall. 
 Alone now, I started trying to assemble options, but very soon heard the toilet flush and she was back. 
 "Quick," I said. 
 "Right down to business," she answered. 
 I closed the hall door, and opened the glass-paned door and we were again the subject of attention.  I said, "We're—" I looked at her. She'd been looking at the spectators, but with my pause she faced me.  I continued, "—going to the movies?" 
 "Sure," she said. Her eyes were blue anywhere. 
 "Nice," said the coach. "Have fun, guys." 
 Out the back door, I opened the passenger door of the Firebird.  She assessed what the dome light revealed and got in. 
 I came around and hopped in and started the car. 
 "Ooh." 
 "I'll let it warm up a bit," I said, setting the manual choke so I wouldn't have to keep my foot on the gas. 
 The dome was off but I turned it back on. 
 She was casually reclined and looking at me.  Between us was a center console made of layered planks of ethafoam I'd pulled from shipping cartons at work. 
 "Okay if I rest my arm on this?" 
 "Uh huh." 
 I put mine on it and flicked the headlight switch, perfectly placed near the shifter.  Her fingers found the Bic in its slot. 
 "You smoke?" 
 "Not cigarettes." 
 "Me neither." She manipulated the lighter with great familiarity. "Well, not for a while now." 
 I reached across to the glove box and pulled out a plastic film canister given to me by someone who owned a camera. Inside was some desiccated bud and a half-finished joint. I brushed the flakes from it and handed it to her. 
 "You roll this?" she asked, sparking the lighter. 
 "Yep," I answered, wondering. 
 She took a cautious hit, rolled down her window, and exhaled toward my housemates' cars.  Then she eased back against the headrest, holding the joint my way. "I like your style." 
 I took a hit and lowered the driver's side window. "What?" 
 "What?" she echoed. 
 "What do you mean? Style." 
 "Oh," she said. "I don't like pinners." 
 "Ah," I said. "Me either. Taste like paper." I snuffed and stowed the J and put the car in reverse, easing down the drive. Through the kitchen window I saw the coach had just filled a glass with water.  He raised it. I flashed him a vee through the windshield. 
 To avoid scraping the tailpipes of the Firebird on the street I'd learned the magic angle to ease out.  The light was only a few houses down and I let the car ride its idle into the left turn lane.  While waiting for the green, I said: "The seat belt is old school." 
 While she figured out it was a non-retracting affair that needed some fiddling to get straight she said: "All the gear . . ." 
 I confirmed: "Every time. Oh, and roll up your window." 
 I heard the buckle click. Her window went up. The light changed. 
 It had taken me a while to figure out that I'm really more interested in cornering than straight line speed.  It had then taken some experimenting, wider wheels, various tires, sway bars, adjustable shocks, to get the big car to do what I wanted. Labors of love are forever works in progress.  Passions are a kind of trap we pay to get into, stay in. Or some such crap. Drop the clutch. 
 "Unhhhh!" 
 It was impressive, especially in first gear, the sudden snarl, the car leaping both forward and sideways.  I preferred to initiate first-time passengers with a left-hand turn to push them against their door instead of toward me. Though one, fearing defenestration, had tried to grab my arm. 
 A shift from first to fourth quieted the cabin. I powered on the stereo. Floyd, "Have a Cigar." 
 "Jesus," she said, rearranging herself. "Do that again." 
 I took a detour to let her experience the only twisty spot in the neighborhood, Dry Creek Road.  I considered it my testing area. Nice houses with fussy landscaping.  Later the city installed traffic signals along there, set to blinking red. Closest I'd get to a statue. 
 The route to the theater had us on El Camino Real. Three lanes wide each way, center turn lane, and breakout right-turn lanes at each intersection. It wasn't a Friday or Saturday night but the Firebird still attracted a friendly challenger at the first stop light. Vroom, vroom. But here she surprised me. 
 "Don't, okay?" 
 I looked at her. "You see a cop?" 
 "No, and I really don't want to." 
 I turned to the guy on my left. "Not tonight, man. The lady isn't into it." 
 He leaned over the wheel of his GTO to have a look, then laughed the laugh of the unencumbered.  We waited through the long cycle, admiring each others' noises. 
 She was watching the cross traffic and when it slowed she put her hand over mine as if to help or prevent me shifting. A picture is worth a thousand words, but a first direct touch holds only a handful of possibilities. Sort them any way you like. 
 On green the guy next to us tore off. It's hard to judge from a docile perspective, couldn't say if I'd've bested him. But maybe I already had. 
 At the cineplex she was definitely my friend now. Holding my hand in the ticket line and the popcorn line.  We watched something while a different movie played in my head. Our hands kept meeting in the paper bucket like friendly dogs at a park. 
 After the show she hit the ladies' and like before was out in record time. 
 "So," I said. 
 "So." She took my hand and we walked out to my car. My inner movie had come up with too many dialog options for me to choose from. 
 "I left my bag at your place." 
 "Right." 
 Back in Willow Glen no one was still up except the cats who were doing their usual after-dark hardwood floors chase-and-skid games. She giggled as they slid past us, bounced off the door and peeled out again. 
 In my bedroom, I turned on my closet light and cleared the motorcycle jackets, helmets and gloves from the mattresses. There was her duffel. 
 I looked at her. 
 "It could stay there," she said, very softly but not whispering. Her eyes were blue in near darkness. 
 "It can." 
 The cats thundered past. 
 "What's the big orange one called?" 
 "Leon." 
 "Good." 
 "What are you called?" 
 The horns curled tight and eased. "Beverly." 
 "That's a nice name." 
 "It means, home of the beaver." 
 My movie script hadn't had that line. 
 "Like," she said, "beaver and then lee, L-E-E." 
 "Huh." 
 "Can I take off my shoes?" 
 "Sure." 
 She sat on the mattress, undid her laces, removed her shoes, stood, and slid out of her shirt and jeans, and then the bikini. Things went everywhere, snowing girl stuff. 
 "Want to take yours off?" she asked. This woman had octaves. 
 "Sure." I wasn't as graceful, but tried to be.  Standing. Boots I could unzip and step out of, but my second sock nearly had me hopping. 
 She crossed her ankles and turned in front of me like a ballerina on a music box.  Vroom. That smile now asymmetrical.  I would learn it went left when waiting and to the right when taking initiative. 
 Morning came with the scrape and soft slam of the back door opening and closing. 
 Out my window I saw the coach get into his Civic. The Fastback was already gone. I'd slept well. I usually do. 
 Beverly was sitting on the end of the mattress reading my journal. It was a large sketch pad because I didn't like to see my thoughts ruled. I used a Fountain Pentel for it's flexible tip. Occasionally, a letter stroke would release tension and with it a delicate spray of blue. 
 "You have nice penmanship." 
 "So do you." Silly, but my intent came across. 
 She closed my journal. Nicely. Virtually in it. 
 At a nearby breakfast joint, as our plates arrived, she asked: "So you can take the day off?" 
 I'd called my office from the place's payphone. "Nothing's on fire." 
 "There, anyway." 
 Eggs Benedict was new for her. First time for everything. I realized I was in the midst of my first time with something, and not just with her specifically. I simply didn't know what it was yet. 
 I said: "How about the beach?" 
 A mouthful of breakfast, hollandaise at the corner, her eyes sparkled like the bay. 
 It wasn't until we were halfway to the coast, the Firebird purring its way up and over the Santa Cruz mountains, that it occurred to me. "I guess you can take a day off working for what's-his-name?"  Dancing around in a bikini watering flowers. 
 She was relighting the joint. Got it going and handed it to me. "Done with that." 
 Ah. That was it. At least a glimpse of it. This, maybe I, was a that. Whatever that meant.  I held the joint and thought about it, then took a fatalistic hit. I'd find out. 
 It was a weekday and traffic was light. I told her I usually skipped past Santa Cruz to head north to a couple of secluded beaches my housemates had shown me, but today wanted to hit the wharf and buy some big towels. 
 She laughted. "You don't have all the gear?" 
 "I did." I told her the story of my necklace, a silver rope holding a rough piece of abalone shell.  The last time I'd been, I'd seen the inch-long magically prismatic fragment tumbling around my ankles in the surf.  It was so elusive I hadn't noticed the sneaker wave that hit me waist-high, knocked me over, and stole my towel, a shoe and my sandwich. But when I sat up, the bit of shell had been in my hand. 
 Lunch on the wharf made shopping easy. I bought her sunglasses and a big hat.  Re-accessorizing to dispel previous ownership.  If her bikini had looked new I'd have helped pick out another.  It didn't and I liked the color on her.  A close call, given its points of contact and possible past. 
 At the car, I put the new towels in the salt-encrusted gym bag that the wave hadn't quite taken, likely because it had been weighed down by a nearly new bottle of sunscreen. 
 I drove north along the coast, noting turnouts where the cliff had been cut nearly to the road by hydrodynamics and time. At one with no cars I pulled across the southbound lane and parked.  The wind was blowing, as always. Trains of brown pelicans were cruising south just offshore.  We picked our way down the cliff to the tiny beach. I spread the new towels, flopped down and said: "Oh, shoot." 
 Beverly smiled and pulled the joint and lighter from her bikini top. 
 We had to get back into the chilly shade of the cliff to get enough out of the wind.  Two hits apiece and it was getting short. I snuffed it against the ragged rock wall. 
 Sitting on her towel under her big hat she remarked: "White caps." 
 "Yeah, I was hoping it'd be a bit warmer. Would be with less wind." 
 She turned. "Here."  She rolled over and onto me, hands on my shoulders, and rubbed her nose against mine. Definitely warmer.  After a few moments she sat up, arching her back to unclip her top and let it slide down her arms.  Her hat was tilted back a bit, points of sky-brightness peeking through intersections of woven straw, a Renaissance halo effect, Saint Ray-Ban With Attendant Areolae. 
 Uncounted pelicans later, we climbed the cliff and reached the top right as another car parked behind mine.  We said hi as they got out and we got in. 
 "Ooh," she said, "ahh," squirming on the passenger seat. I was glad I'd put sheepskin covers over the vinyl.  Completely changed the sun-baked Firebird experience. 
 I stopped at the taqueria on the edge of town. I broke my policy and got a Corona with my carne asada plate, but only drank half. She finished hers and fell asleep on the drive home. 
 And home it apparently was now.  And I wasn't sure what to do about that.  My housemates and I had had significant others in the past, but since I'd moved in there'd been dating but no sleepovers.  It seemed she had no where else to be, or perhaps go.  I knew the difference between being alone and a loner.  But how to tell a stray from one astray? 
 "Do we need to pick up any stuff from anywhere?" 
 She looked at me as if she'd known this polite question had to have been a red flag on my horizon.  If I'd been hearing any lines til now, this one sounded the most over-thought: "Nah. I travel light." 
 It begged other questions while implying they'd be best unasked. 
 That was part of this. 
 Another part presented itself almost immediately.  I was grabbing a couple of Cokes from the fridge and saw the Civic and the Fastback go past the window toward the back. Coincidence. Or maybe they'd met after work somewhere. I wondered what for. 
 The back door opened as I handed Beverly a can. We'd been watching MTV with the cats. 
 "Hey," the coach said, to me, "can you take a look at this car thing?" To her he said, "Shouldn't take long." No tone, the dude was cool. But. 
 Outside, it was clear there was no car thing. 
 The coach said to me: "So. Things going well?" 
 "Yeah." 
 "Thing is," said my other housemate, Leon's owner, Mr. Fastback, "like, who is she?" 
 "Beverly," I said. 
 There was a pause. I said, "We need some locks on the garage."  Careful now. No guilt. A favor. "I can take care of that for us." 
 "When?" he asked. "She seems to just be here." 
 A day. I said: "I know." And I did. "But I think she understands the situation." 
 "Do you?" 
 I paused again. I checked my voice. "Well, if we need to establish some rules, we can.  I understand if a fourth person starts staying here, that's a thing." 
 "Yeah," he snorted. 
 "So," I said, "I'm prepared to pay a larger perentage of rent, if—" 
 "It's not that," he said. "I've got—" and he thumbed toward the garage. 
 "Well," I said, "she smokes weed like us. I don't see her calling the cops." 
 "But what about friends?" 
 "Like, thieves? I'll admit I don't know her that well. But if she starts to act fishy—" 
 "It'd happen fast," he interrupted. "She'll call someone while we're all out, and—" 
 "Right, so I'll put locks on the doors." His chickens, his farm. But, no, I'd benefit, too. 
 The coach had been standing by, all Californian. "So," he asked Mr. Fastback, "will that do it?" 
 "What about my stereo?" 
 "Look," I said, "I'll replace anything that goes missing. Gosh, these concerns seem to have come up pretty quick. I mean, she's only been here one night—" 
 "Is she staying tonight?" Fastback pressed. 
 "Unless I'm going to take her to a hotel," I answered. I checked myself again.  "Tell you what, guys. Let's all go to dinner." 
 The coach started nodding. Dude burned a million calories in the pool every day. 
 I went on: "She'll be less of an unknown. You'll have a better read on her. Or did she already give a bad vibe or . . ." 
 "Not for me," said the coach. 
 "She's friends with the cats," I said. Breathe, be cool. 
 "Let's go to the burger place," said the coach. "I could go for their Chili Size." 
 Mr. Fastback was parked easiest. "You drive?" I asked him. 
 He nodded, seeking solace in his fedora, disappointed his freak-out was being short-circuited so soon.  As for the coach, I don't think he had any real worries, not owning anything worth stealing. How much can you get for a second-hand Speedo? 
 As I turned back toward the house I found myself wondering how they even knew she'd spent the night.  Then I remembered her giggling at the cats. And some other noises. Jealous, Mr. Fastback.  If you aren't having fun, no one can. But as I turned the knob, on another level, yep, driving into fog. 
 "Hey," I said to Beverly, "we're heading out for burgers." 
 She powered off the TV and moved the cats. They were sharing her lap. Wise guys. 
 I popped the passenger door of the Fastback and tilted the seat forward so Beverly and I could get to the backseat bench. There was a hole in the vinyl on the driver side about the size of a dollar bill revealing the horizontal wavy seat springs, one of which had a missing section.  Beneath the springs was a piece of plywood. 
 The coach flipped the passenger seat forward and got in. Mr. Fastback got behind the wheel. 
 I told her: "Ask our driver how that hole came to be." 
 She peered at him in the mirror as he backed down the driveway. 
 "Ah," he said. "Yeah. Well. Me and—my ex were, uh—" 
 "Getting heavy," the coach said. 
 "—yeah. And it turns out that the battery is right underneath the seat. So when two people . . .  with two people's weight, it was enough to . . ." He was smirking and snorking by now. 
 The coach finished: "To make the seat springs make contact with the battery terminals." 
 Beverly crowded her jeans against mine, wary of the melted vinyl.  She peeked again at the rearview and started laughing. A rich contralto, highly contagious.  It went on for the first block or so. She ran down, then covered her mouth and started again.  We were wiping our eyes when we got to the place. As she was getting out she gave the hole and springs another look and asked: "Anybody get branded?" 
 I'd never thought to ask. 
 Mr. Fastback turned pink as Bev's bikini. 
 We were seated in a booth, were holding menus, and crying so hard we could hardly order.
